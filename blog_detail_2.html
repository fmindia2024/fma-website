Context as Catalyst

February 2, 2026 • 5 min read

A building does not exist in a vacuum. It is anchored to a specific point on earth, with a unique history, topography, and climate. At FMA, we reject the notion of 'tabula rasa'—the blank slate. Instead, we view the site as a manuscript already half-written. Our job is not to erase what is there, but to contribute a new chapter that resonates with the existing narrative.

True sustainability goes beyond solar panels and efficiency ratings. It begins with a deep listening to the land. How does the wind move through the valley? Where does the sun hit first in winter? What are the local vernacular traditions, and why did they evolve that way?

The Soil Tells a Story

Before we draw a single line, we spend time on the land. We look at the soil composition, the native flora, and the surrounding structures. In a recent project in the highlands, we utilized rammed earth extracted directly from the foundation excavation. This not only reduced material transport but visually tied the house to the horizon. The walls literally rose from the ground beneath them.

“Architecture should not simply sit upon the land, but grow out of it.”

Climate as a Design Partner

Modern technology allows us to build glass boxes in the desert or uninsulated shacks in the snow, maintained by massive energy consumption. We believe this is a failure of imagination. Architecture should be passive. It should work with the climate, not fight against it. Deep overhangs, cross-ventilation corridors, and thermal mass are timeless tools that we deploy to create comfort without artificial dependence.

Cultural Context

Context is also human. It is the culture of the people who live there. We look at local craft traditions—stone masonry, weaving, timber framing—and find ways to reinterpret them contemporarily. This honors the past without mimicking it. It creates a sense of belonging, ensuring the building feels like a native inhabitant of its place.

When architecture respects its context, it becomes timeless. It feels inevitable, as if it has always been there, and always will be.
